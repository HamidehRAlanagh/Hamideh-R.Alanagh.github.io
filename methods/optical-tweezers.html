Optical Tweezers Microrheology in Coacervate Research

Optical tweezers microrheology is a powerful technique used to measure the viscoelastic properties of soft matter by tracking the motion of microbeads trapped in laser beams. This method allows for the application of controlled forces and real-time tracking of particle displacement, making it particularly suited for characterizing the mechanical behavior of condensates, gels, and vesicles across a broad frequency range. It enables both passive (thermal fluctuations) and active (controlled perturbation) measurements, capturing dynamic responses of soft materials with high spatial and temporal resolution.

In my research, I use optical tweezers to quantify the mechanics of recombinant Mfp-1 (rMfp-1) protein coacervates. By embedding a single bead inside a droplet and applying a multiharmonic force, I obtain frequency-dependent storage and loss moduli across physiologically relevant pH and salt conditions. These measurements help us understand how mussel-inspired coacervates transition from fluid-like to more elastic phases in response to environmental cues—insights that are vital for bioadhesive design and synthetic compartment engineering.

I also extend this method to study the mechanics of vesicles by trapping beads attached to vesicle membranes. This allows us to explore vesicle elasticity and membrane mechanics in configurations similar to AFM-based indentation, providing complementary mechanical readouts for complex, layered systems.

Altogether, optical tweezers microrheology supports the development of responsive biomaterials by revealing how their molecular architecture translates into emergent mechanical behavior.
