NMR Spectroscopy in Coacervate Research

To understand the molecular interactions governing coacervate formation and mechanical properties, I use solution-state NMR spectroscopy, particularly 1D 1H and DOSY NMR techniques. These experiments help us capture how rMfp-1 condensates respond to varying salt types and concentrations.

For instance, when NaCl or Na2SO4 is added to a concentrated protein solution, we observe pronounced peak broadening in the aromatic region of the 1H NMR spectrum—indicative of coacervate formation. In contrast, dilute or non-coacervated samples do not exhibit this broadening. This provides a non-invasive, label-free signature of phase separation.

By examining how spectra evolve over time, especially as the dense phase settles or is remixed, we can track the reversibility of coacervation. DOSY NMR further confirms the presence of two diffusing species: a fast-diffusing dilute phase and a much slower-diffusing dense phase, with diffusion coefficients differing by an order of magnitude.

Expanding the Role of NMR in Coacervate Research

Looking ahead, NMR offers even broader possibilities. Isotope labeling (e.g., 13C, 15N) can allow residue-specific resolution, enabling the study of interaction hotspots and secondary structure formation within droplets. Relaxation experiments and multidimensional NMR could provide detailed information on protein backbone dynamics and transient binding events—both crucial for understanding liquid–liquid phase separation (LLPS) mechanisms and their regulation.

In combination with Raman, AFM, or ToF-SIMS, NMR serves as a vital tool in constructing a holistic picture of coacervate structure, dynamics, and function from the molecular to the mesoscopic scale.
