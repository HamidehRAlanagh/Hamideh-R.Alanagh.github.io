ToF-SIMS in Coacervate Research

In my research on protein coacervates and bioinspired materials, I use Time-of-Flight Secondary Ion Mass Spectrometry (ToF-SIMS) to gather detailed, label-free chemical information at the microscale and below. The technique excels at capturing the spatial distribution of molecules and ions across soft, hydrated, and often compositionally heterogeneous systems like coacervates.

How ToF-SIMS Contributes

Surface Chemistry at High Resolution

One of the strengths of ToF-SIMS is its ability to probe the outermost nanometers of a sample. In coacervate research, this helps me identify where certain ions, counterions, or ligands are concentrated. I can assess how surface chemistry varies across different domains, which is especially useful when working with structures like core–shell droplets or materials interacting with membranes.

Mapping Composition Without Labels

Coacervates are chemically rich systems that may contain mixtures of proteins, nucleic acids, and other components. ToF-SIMS provides detailed chemical maps without the need for fluorescent tags or external probes. This makes it possible to trace molecular gradients, distinguish protein-rich from nucleic acid-rich regions, or observe how the chemistry shifts in response to salt, pH, or redox conditions.

Ion-Specific and Isotopic Sensitivity

ToF-SIMS is well-suited for detecting both common elements (like C, N, O) and trace metals (like Fe, Zn, or Ca) that play a role in coacervate formation or stabilization. I also use it to investigate molecular modifications or to follow isotopically labeled components that help track diffusion, degradation, or uptake events over time.

Correlations with Structure

By integrating ToF-SIMS with structural tools like Atomic Force Microscopy (AFM) or cryo-electron microscopy, I can relate physical features—such as stiffness gradients or interface sharpness—to underlying chemistry. This integrated view is important for understanding how molecular organization gives rise to mechanical or dynamic behavior.

Broader Applications

Beyond coacervates, I also apply ToF-SIMS to study protein–surface interactions, environmentally relevant interfaces, and composite biomaterials. The method helps me visualize adsorbed protein layers, understand adhesion mechanisms, and explore how ions like calcium or iron influence the organization of phase-separated assemblies.

Ultimately, ToF-SIMS gives me the ability to peer into the chemical detail of soft matter systems—capturing both the subtle and the spatial—without disrupting their structure.
