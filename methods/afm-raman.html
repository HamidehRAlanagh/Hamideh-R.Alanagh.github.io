AFM–Raman Spectroscopy in Coacervate Research

To study the architecture and behavior of protein coacervates, I use a combined Atomic Force Microscopy–Raman Spectroscopy (AFM–Raman) platform. The integrated system I work with (HORIBA) enables simultaneous acquisition of topographical and chemical maps, making it particularly useful for soft, heterogeneous systems like protein-based condensates.

AFM provides nanometer-scale information about surface structure and mechanical stiffness, while Raman spectroscopy delivers molecular fingerprints of chemical bonds, hydration, and secondary structure. By correlating these datasets, I can directly connect morphology to molecular composition—for instance, linking a core–shell structure to gradients in protein conformation or water content.

AFM–Raman has proven valuable in coacervate systems because it doesn’t require staining, operates under ambient or liquid-compatible conditions, and can be used to follow coacervate dynamics over time. This helps us understand how environmental stimuli—such as changes in pH or salt concentration—modulate material properties in situ.

In my recent work, I use AFM–Raman to examine how tyrosine-rich proteins reorganize upon phase separation. Raman signals reveal differences in hydration and molecular interactions across the coacervate, while AFM detects variations in stiffness that align with these chemical gradients.

Looking ahead, this technique opens up possibilities for understanding biomolecular organization in other systems where phase behavior is tightly coupled to function, including synthetic organelles, underwater adhesives, or compartmentalized reaction platforms. With its ability to bridge structure and chemistry, AFM–Raman offers a unique window into the mesoscale physics of soft matter.
