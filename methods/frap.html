FRAP in Coacervate Research

Fluorescence Recovery After Photobleaching (FRAP) is a valuable tool for probing molecular mobility in soft matter systems. By selectively photobleaching a region of interest within a fluorescent sample and tracking the return of fluorescence over time, FRAP provides insight into the diffusion dynamics of biomolecules. This approach is particularly useful for studying the internal fluidity and molecular interactions within phase-separated condensates.

In my research, I use FRAP to evaluate the dynamic properties of coacervates. We incorporate a small fraction of dye-labeled protein and monitor fluorescence recovery following photobleaching. This enables us to quantify two key parameters: the mobile and immobile fractions of the protein, and the time required for fluorescence recovery—which is directly related to protein diffusion.

For example, our findings show that sulfate-induced coacervates have a higher immobile fraction than chloride-induced coacervates across all tested pH values. As pH increases, the mobility of rMfp-1 within droplets decreases, suggesting enhanced intermolecular interactions or cross-linking. Interestingly, while FRAP recovery times are similar between 400 mM Na2SO4 and 600 mM NaCl, our microrheology measurements show marked differences in viscoelastic relaxation times. This highlights the complementary nature of the two techniques: FRAP reflects local protein mobility, while microrheology captures bulk mechanical behavior.

Taken together, FRAP allows us to visualize how molecular dynamics are modulated by environmental conditions such as pH and ionic strength. These insights are critical for understanding the functional properties of protein coacervates, particularly in systems inspired by biological adhesives and compartmentalized architectures.
