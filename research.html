Collagen Liquid Crystal Structures

Research Period: 2023-2025

Status: Published

This research involved formulating collagen liquid crystal structures specifically designed for native fibril-zed tissue fabrication. The work focused on understanding and controlling the orientation of collagen structures using a comprehensive suite of analytical techniques.

The study employed Polarized Raman Spectroscopy to examine molecular orientation, rheology to characterize flow properties, SAXS and WAXS for structural analysis at different length scales, and cryo-EM for high-resolution imaging of the liquid crystal structures.

Key Techniques: Polarized Raman Spectroscopy, rheology, SAXS, WAXS, cryo-EM

Impact: Advanced understanding of collagen liquid crystal behavior for tissue engineering applications

Native Mussel Foot Protein Vesicle Mechanics

Research Period: 2022-2024

Status: Published

This foundational work involved separating native vesicles from mussel foot glands, specifically identifying and isolating core, cuticle, and plaque vesicles. I developed an optical tweezers method to measure the mechanical properties of these vesicles, revealing how their mechanics change as a function of pH and the orientation of applied force.

The study provided crucial insights into the natural mechanical properties of mussel foot protein vesicles and how environmental conditions affect their behavior. This work established the foundation for understanding the mechanical adaptability of natural protein-based materials.

Key Techniques: Optical tweezers microrheology, vesicle separation, pH-dependent mechanics

Impact: Established baseline understanding of native mussel foot protein vesicle mechanics

Mussel Foot Protein (MFP) Coacervates

Research Period: 2023-2024

Status: Published in Chemistry of Materials, 2025

This foundational work demonstrated how varying ionic conditions, specifically using multivalent sulfate ions, alters phase boundaries and increases coacervate stiffness and concentration. I measured these effects with optical tweezers microrheology, which provides mechanical measurements at the single-droplet level, and FRAP, which tracks molecular mobility inside the coacervate.

These measurements demonstrated that mechanical properties can be adjusted across a range of values, supporting processing applications such as fiber formation and coatings. This work established the foundation for understanding how ionic environments control coacervate properties.

Key Techniques: Optical tweezers microrheology, FRAP, single-droplet analysis

Impact: Established tunability principles for coacervate mechanical properties

Protein–Cellulose Condensates

Research Period: 2024

Status: Submitted 2025, Presented at CSC 2025 Ottawa

I developed protein–cellulose condensates that form porous structures without added salts. These assemblies rely on electrostatic and hydrogen-bonding interactions. Using AFM-Raman spectroscopy, I mapped chemical and mechanical properties, showing anisotropic, permeable interfaces.

Cryo-EM imaging confirmed consistent nanoscale architectures. This work demonstrates how different interaction mechanisms can create distinct structural features in coacervate systems, expanding the toolkit for materials design.

Key Techniques: AFM-Raman spectroscopy, Cryo-EM, chemical mapping

Impact: Demonstrated salt-free assembly pathways for porous materials

MFP–DNA Complex Coacervates

Research Period: 2024-2025

Status: In preparation, Presented at CSC 2025 Ottawa

In studies of MFP–DNA complex coacervates, I examined how sequence-specific electrostatic and hydrophobic interactions affect phase stability and internal fluidity. FRAP measurements showed that internal diffusion rates can change significantly based on sequence design, allowing control over cargo encapsulation and release.

I used NMR spectroscopy, Raman, and TOF-SIMS to characterize local chemical environments and interfacial composition. This work demonstrates the potential for sequence-programmable coacervate systems.

Key Techniques: FRAP, NMR spectroscopy, Raman, TOF-SIMS

Impact: Demonstrated sequence-dependent control over coacervate function

Tunable Coacervate Materials Design

Proposed Period: 2026-2027

Objective: Synthesize and characterize protein–nucleic acid coacervates with precisely controlled mechanical, chemical, and structural properties to enable selective cargo encapsulation, targeted release, and tailored functionality in sustainable materials applications.

Approach: Building on our established understanding of ionic effects and sequence-specific interactions, we will develop systematic design rules for creating coacervates with predictable properties.

Key Methods: Recombinant protein expression, automated nucleic acid synthesis, advanced characterization using ToF-SIMS, AFM-Raman, and NMR

Expected Impact: Enable rational design of coacervate materials for specific applications
