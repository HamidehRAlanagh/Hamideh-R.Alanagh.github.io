Environmental Chemistry

My environmental chemistry research spans materials design for sustainable agriculture, water purification, and understanding the behavior of emerging contaminants like micro- and nanoplastics. I integrate fundamental tools from soft matter and surface science with applied environmental needs, aiming to inform sustainable and safe solutions through cross-disciplinary collaboration.

Protein-Based Nanostructures for Plant Nutrient Delivery

As a postdoctoral fellow in Prof. Subhasis Ghoshal’s lab, I have developed a two-dimensional protein-based nanostructured platform for targeted nutrient delivery in plants. These systems enhance nutrient-use efficiency and minimize environmental losses, contributing to more sustainable agricultural practices while addressing concerns of soil health and fertilizer overuse.

Microplastic Fate and Heavy Metal Interactions

In collaboration with Prof. Audrey Moores (Green Chemistry, McGill), I explored the environmental persistence and metal-binding properties of aged microplastics in freshwater systems. We used nano Time-of-Flight Secondary Ion Mass Spectrometry (ToF-SIMS) to analyze UV-aged polyethylene microplastics and map how biofilm formation and polymer oxidation influence the adsorption of heavy metals like Pb and Cd.

Tracking Selenium-Doped Nanoplastics in Aquatic Systems

Together with Prof. Nathalie Tufenkji (Environmental Engineering, McGill), I studied the behavior of irregularly shaped selenium-doped nanoplastics as tracers for plastic fragmentation and ecotoxicological fate. Using nano ToF-SIMS, we followed selenium signals to understand their transport in complex aquatic matrices, offering a more realistic view of micro- and nanoplastic impacts in natural environments.

These studies emphasize the importance of integrated materials–environment research in addressing questions of ecological safety, nutrient recovery, and contaminant dynamics.
