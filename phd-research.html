Biomedical Applications of Porous Materials

During my Ph.D., I approached biomedical applications of porous materials by first understanding the fundamental mechanisms, then engineering solutions, and finally optimizing for practical use. For intracellular delivery of Cytochrome C using MOFs, I needed to understand not just the synthesis, but how pore architecture affects protein stability during apoptosome formation, how the materials interact with cellular environments, and what this means for apoptosis-induced cancer treatment efficacy (data unpublished).

Similarly, when working on controlled release systems, I didn't just observe that Oxytocin (OT) follows zero-order release kinetics through COF membranes - I investigated why this happens by examining how membrane crystallinity and pore ordering influence molecular transport.1

During my time as a visiting scholar at the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign, I approached the design of a MOF-based theranostic stent by considering the complete system requirements: biocompatibility, mechanical properties, drug loading capacity, and diagnostic functionality. Rather than simply incorporating MOFs into existing stent designs, I redesigned the architecture to leverage the unique properties these materials offer.2

My investigation of MOF-biliverdin interactions exemplifies this comprehensive approach. I used photoacoustic and fluorescence techniques to observe the interactions, employed DFT calculations to understand the electron transfer mechanisms, and then connected these molecular-level insights to potential applications in photodynamic therapy.3

The poly(L-lactide) diffusion study followed similar logic - I observed enhanced crystallization through solid-state NMR, understood the mechanism behind the shape memory effects, and considered how this knowledge could inform composite design principles.4

Environmentally Conscious Synthesis

While working on coordinated polymer synthesis during my Ph.D., I recognized that developing new materials without considering their environmental impact was shortsighted. This realization led me to systematically address the sustainability challenge by developing water-based synthesis routes as alternatives to traditional organic solvents. But I didn't stop at green synthesis - I also needed to ensure these materials could match or exceed the performance of conventionally synthesized ones.

By incorporating these water-synthesized metal-coordinated polymers into epoxy composites, I demonstrated that environmental responsibility doesn't require performance compromises.5

I then extended this thinking to explore other applications, finding that these biomineralized porous polymers work effectively as heterogeneous catalysts for CO₂ reduction (data unpublished).

This integrated approach—understanding fundamental properties, optimizing performance, and considering practical applications—has resulted in patent applications in Iran, China, and the US (currently under evaluation). More importantly, it provided insights into how design choices at the molecular level (like atomically smooth pores in COF channels) influence system-level performance (diffusion rates and overall efficiency).

International Collaborative Research

Beyond my primary research projects, I've collaborated with researchers across Iran, China, Switzerland, and the US on various projects spanning both biomaterial and non-biomaterial applications. These collaborations have included work on Organic Solvent Nanofiltration (OSN)9, CO₂ reduction systems10, supercapacitor development11, and chirality studies12, as well as various biomaterial-related projects focusing on advanced therapeutic delivery systems and imaging applications.6–8,13

This collaborative experience demonstrates my ability to contribute meaningfully to diverse research ecosystems while maintaining focus on materials innovation with practical impact.
